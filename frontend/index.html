Project Context & Hypotheses

Transformer language models are trained to predict the next token, yet they consistently produce grammatically well-formed sentences. This raises an important question in computational linguistics: do these models internally learn formal grammatical structure, or do they simply mimic surface patterns? Our project investigates whether the internal attention patterns of transformer models naturally align with dependency tree structures.

Research Questions

  • Do specialized syntactic attention heads emerge consistently across different languages?
  • Is the model more accurate at identifying core grammatical relations (e.g., subjects, objects) than peripheral modifiers?

Working Hypotheses

  • Core arguments (nsubj, obj) will achieve higher Unlabeled Attachment Scores (UAS) than adjunct relations like advcl or nmod.
  • Language typology heavily affects syntactic emergence mapping (e.g., head-final Hindi/Japanese vs head-initial English).

Research Findings & Analysis

The Core Concept: Modern language models do not merely memorize statistical word frequencies; they learn deep structural hierarchies. By probing the self-attention weights inside BERT, we can extract Directed Acyclic Graphs that strongly correlate with traditional linguistic dependency trees.

For each Universal Dependency relation, we evaluate all 144 attention heads (12 layers × 12 heads) individually to identify a "specialist" head that aligns most accurately with a specific syntactic relationship (e.g., adjectival modifiers). Model 1 evaluates this phenomena organically using these specialist heads, discovered purely from a 70% training split of the corpus.

Key Observation: Middle-to-higher layers (mostly layers 5-8) tend to dominate for structural relations like amod, det, and case, suggesting BERT builds generalized syntactic features in the middle of its progression. Conversely, weak structural ties like punct score consistently poorly, implying attention isn't structurally bound by punctuation.

Rare relations making up less than 0.5% of total corpus word tokens were excluded to preserve statistical robustness.

Model 1 — Average UAS by Language (Held-Out Test Set)

To validate that our specialist heads weren't simply overfitting to the training set, we evaluate the Unlabeled Attachment Score (UAS) exclusively on the held-out 30% test split. The UAS represents the pure percentage of words attached to their correct syntactic parent, utilizing our derived zero-shot head averages.

Model UAS vs Sentence Length — Language Comparison

As sentences grow longer, the total pool of candidate parent tokens linearly expands, naturally making dependency resolution exponentially harder. The solid colored lines trace smoothed moving averages of our Model's UAS at exact sentence lengths.

The Random Baseline: Crucially, notice the dashed line representing the mathematical expectation (1/n) of achieving a correct parent attachment if the choice inherently relied on a random graph. The model significantly elevates over this boundary, demonstrating that it genuinely grasps profound grammatical structure over mere random likelihood.
