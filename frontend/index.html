Do LLMs Develop Dependency Grammar?

Investigating whether transformer attention heads internally learn formal grammatical structures or simply mimic surface patterns.

Project Context & Hypotheses

Transformer language models are trained to predict the next token, yet they consistently produce grammatically well-formed sentences. This raises an important question in computational linguistics: do these models internally learn formal grammatical structure, or do they simply mimic surface patterns? Our project investigates whether the internal attention patterns of transformer models naturally align with dependency tree structures.

Research Questions

  • Do specialized syntactic attention heads emerge consistently across different languages?
  • Is the model more accurate at identifying core grammatical relations (e.g., subjects, objects) than peripheral modifiers?

Working Hypotheses

  • Core arguments (nsubj, obj) will achieve higher Unlabeled Attachment Scores (UAS) than adjunct relations like advcl or nmod.
  • Language typology heavily affects syntactic emergence mapping (e.g., head-final Hindi/Japanese vs head-initial English).

Research Findings & Analysis

How the experiment works: We feed sentences through bert-base-multilingual-cased and record the internal self-attention weights — numbers that describe how much each word is "attending to" every other word. We then check: does the word receiving the most attention from token i match the syntactic parent assigned by linguists in the Universal Dependencies corpus?

To find the best signal, we evaluate all 144 attention heads (12 layers × 12 heads) individually on a 70% training split. For each UD relation type (e.g., nsubj, amod), the head with the highest correct-parent hit rate becomes its Specialist Head. The model is then evaluated zero-shot on a completely separate 30% test split.

Statistical Validation — Linear Mixed Model

Simply comparing average UAS across relations would be misleading — longer sentences and cross-linguistic variation both independently affect attention weights. To isolate the pure grammatical signal, we fit a Linear Mixed Model:

y ∼ β0 + β1T + β2L + (1|Language) + ε

  • y — attention weight of the child→parent token pair
  • T — gold dependency type (fixed effect, e.g. nsubj, obj)
  • L — sentence length in tokens (confounder, controlled away)
  • (1|Language) — random intercept per language (absorbs cross-lingual baseline differences)

The coefficients β1 in the Forest Plot below reveal which dependency types genuinely drive higher attention weights beyond what sentence length or language typology alone would predict.

Language Typology Effects — (1|Language) Random Intercepts

The (1|Language) term in the LMM captures each language's individual baseline shift in attention weight, after the fixed effects of dependency type and sentence length are removed. This directly tests the proposal's hypothesis about language typology:

  • A positive intercept means the language systematically produces higher attention weights on gold-standard parent tokens than the global average — i.e., dependency structure is more easily visible in attention.
  • A negative intercept means attention is more distributed, making the correct parent harder to recover from attention alone.

Head-final languages like Japanese and Hindi (SOV word order) tend to show different intercepts from head-initial English (SVO), confirming that language typology does affect where and how strongly syntactic information is encoded in BERT's attention.
